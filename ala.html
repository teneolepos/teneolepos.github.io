Motivating Individuals to get Screened

www.savedbythescan.com

Strategy / UX / Product Management

The American Lung Association and Ad Council wanted to raise awareness about lung cancer screening now that a new, effective procedure is available. They wanted a campaign strategy and execution across TV, print and digital.

We concepted the Saved By The Scan campaign, and I worked with a team of eight across creative, interaction design, development, product management. I created the wireframes, conducted user testing, and provided guidance in content creation, design and development.

Impact

  • Since launch, 33,000+ people have taken the quiz to get screened
  • Three days after launch, we had 100 users in one instance taking the quiz
  • Time Magazine wrote a piece about the campaign and website
  • Al Roker also tweeted about our campaign

IA and Site Map

The key metrics for the Saved By The Scan campaign were:

  • the number of people taking a quiz to determine if they should get screened
  • the number of people downloading the doctor discussion guide
  • the number of people sharing and spreading the word about the screening

All of this helped me identify the primary content for the “microsite”. I created and presented a sitemap consisting of four core pages: Take the Quiz, Getting Screened, Stories, and Resources.

I then reviewed and edited copy with the Creative team to make sure we used friendly and direct language to provide the most relevant information upfront. Where the old pages about screening were lengthy and convoluted, the new content was informative and easily digestable so the user knew the important details about screening to take action.

Design

The challenge for the microsite was to create a better, more engaging experience that didn’t disassociate it from lung.org. I was also constrained by the templates in the CMS and global elements in the parent site that should stay the same to maintain consistency.

Given this, I chose to differentiate the campaign pages by implementing a more responsive design that better used white space and a simplified grid layout. This also supported the new content and how we spoke to users. I then worked with our UI designer to create hi-fidelity mockups of the final design. We used a simplified color palette and finer design elements to create a cleaner, more modern look and feel.

Development

The hi-fi mockups were then passed onto our developers. This made development a lot more seamless since they already knew what they were building towards. It also gave them specs and ratios for each design element.

I provided guidance on the intended functionality for the interactions laid out in the wires. I also wrote and revised stories for our developers in Jira.

One of the biggest improvements I made during this phase was to simplify the quiz to increase completion rates for users. By combining similar questions and using more human language, I designed a better experience and helped shorten development time.

User Testing

After launch, I conducted a rapid round of user testing using a third party tool, Validately. The purpose was just to do a “disaster check” for the desktop and mobile versions of the site. The tool screened well so that all testers fit the demographic of people who were at risk and could qualify for screening.

I created five task based scenarios to check navigation, content, functionality, and shareability. I also asked five follow-up questions around general design and key campaign metrics (how likely were users to take the quiz, get screened and talk to their doctor after visiting the site).

After reviewing the video recordings, I identified two major issues. First, the navigation copy we used to direct users to the quiz was not clear. Users were clicking on other pages to get to the quiz. Second, the quiz results content was not centered within the user’s viewport on page load. Users often missed the actual outcome of the quiz and were confused when the first thing they saw was the supporting information beneath it. These content and code changes were quick fixes that were able to make a big impact on the site experience.
